    The FingerReader makes it possible

    What is it?

    The FingerReader is a text-reading assistive device for the blind. It is designed to be portable and assist blind people in reading text in everyday situations.

    With just the point of a finger, visually impaired users can have menus, books, forms, and more read to them in real time. The device gives the user the freedom to read full paragraphs, scan through lines to identify content of interest, and navigate through multiple pages with ease.

    The device provides vibration and tonal feedbacks to help guide the user through text. A vibration signal is triggered at the beginning and end of each line, and tones help alert the user whenever he or she strays off of the line.

    The Design Process

    The ring is 3D printed using dual-materials. The rubber material on the sides of the ring allows for flexible sizing and vibration dampening. The design was tailored using feedback from users to maximize comfort and haptic feedback distinguishability. An on-board microcontroller enables vibration feedbacks and smart LEDs for reading in dark areas. A camera on the device streams video of the text to the processer, and the text is converted into speech so blind users can hear exactly what is on the page in front of them.

    Lessons Learned

    The importance of user feedback: Without our generous users, FingerReader would be no more than a cool idea. Bringing iteration after iteration of the device to our users for feedback greatly influenced the final design. The FingerReader would be nothing without this feedback.


    Quick prototyping: I was in charge of designing the form factor and interactions for the FingerReader. In order to keep with the tight prototyping schedule, I elicited feedback from co-workers in between meetings with the blind users. This allowed for quick changes to be made throughout the process. For example, the use of rubberized material on the ring was a product of feedback from a co-worker that the vibration signals were not distinguishable enough. The rubber material was added to dampen these vibrations.


    Arduino Programming: All feedbacks were enabled through an Arduino. Apart from the camera that was separately powered via USB, the vibration motors, light sensors, and LEDs were all controlled by the Arduino.


    Designing for 3D Printing: The FingerReader served as a valuable lesson in tolerancing. With multiple 3D printers used throughout the project, I constantly adjusted tolerances for ring sizing, fit of electronics, and overall robustness of the design.
