    The FingerReader makes it possible

    What is it?

    The FingerReader is a text-reading assistive device for the blind. It is designed to be a portable device that can assist blind people in reading text in everyday situations.

    With just the point of a finger, visually impaired users can have menus, books, forms, and more read to them in real time. The device allows the user the freedom to read full paragraphs, scan through lines to identify content of interest, and navigate through multiple pages with ease.

    The device provides vibration and tonal feedbacks to help guide the user through text. A vibration signal is triggered at the beginning and end of each line, and tones help alert the user whenever he or she strays off of the line.

    The Design Process

    The ring is a dual-material 3D printed device that allows for sizing adjustments and vibration dampening with the rubber material on the sides of the ring. Through multiple user studies, the design was tailored to maximize user comfort and distinguishability of haptic feedbacks. An on-board microcontroller enables vibration feedbacks and LEDs for reading in dark areas. A camera on the device streams video of the text to the processor that then converts text to speech so blind users can hear exactly what is on the page in front of them.

    Lessons Learned

    The importance of user feedback: Without our generous users, FingerReader would be no more than a potentially cool idea. Being able to bring iteration after iteration to our users fpr feedback was what brought about the final user interactions for the device. The FingerReader would be nothing without this feedback.


    High speed prototyping: Throughout the process, I was in charge of designing the form factor and interactions for the device. In order to keep with the tight prototyping schedule, I elicited feedback from co-workers in between meetings with the users. This allowed for quick changes to be made throughout the process. For example, the use of rubberized material on the sides of the ring was a product of feedback from a co-worker that the vibration signals were not distinguishable enough. The rubber material was added to dampen these vibrations.


    Arduino Programming: All feedbacks were enabled through Arduino. Apart from the camera that was separately powered via USB, the vibration motors, light sensors, and LED add-ons were all enabled through Arduino programming.


    Designing for 3D Printing: The FingerReader served as a valuable lesson in tolerancing. With multiple 3D printers used throughout the project, I had to constantly adjust tolerances for ring sizing, fit of electronics, and overall robustness of the design.
